Grammar Focus

Master the modal verbs and structures used to give and ask for advice.

SHOULD / OUGHT TO → Advice

Subject + should / ought to + base verb

→ You should study harder for the Bac exam.

→ You ought to see a doctor about that.

⚠ "Ought to" is slightly more formal than "should" and is less common in questions.

IF I WERE YOU → Conditional Advice

If I were you, I would + base verb

→ If I were you, I would talk to your parents first.

→ If I were in your shoes, I'd apologise immediately.

📌 This is a Conditional Type 2 structure. Notice: "were" (not "was") is used even for I/he/she.

WHY DON'T YOU / HOW ABOUT → Suggestions

Why don't you + base verb?

→ Why don't you apply for a scholarship?

How about + -ing?

→ How about talking to your teacher?

COULD / MIGHT → Tentative Advice

Subject + could / might + base verb

→ You could try a different approach.

→ You might want to reconsider your decision.

💡 "Could" and "might" make advice sound less imposing — ideal in formal/polite contexts.

YOU'D BETTER → Warning / Urgent Advice ⭐

Subject + had better ('d better) + base verb

→ You'd better start revising now or you'll fail.

→ She'd better see a doctor — that cough sounds serious.

Negative: Subject + had better not + base verb

→ You'd better not ignore this problem any longer.

⚠ "Had better" implies a warning or consequence — stronger than "should". It does NOT refer to the past despite having "had". Use it for urgent, immediate situations. It is NOT followed by "to".

IT'S (HIGH) TIME → Urgent / Overdue Advice

It's time / It's high time + subject + past simple

→ It's time you started taking your studies seriously.

→ It's high time she apologised to her friend.

Alternative: It's time + to + base verb (less personal)

→ It's time to make a decision.

⚠ Key rule: After It's time / It's high time + subject, always use the past simple — NOT the infinitive.
✗ It's high time you to study.
✓ It's high time you studied.

💡 "High time" adds extra urgency — it means the action is long overdue and should have happened already. It often carries a tone of mild reproach.

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue carefully. Notice how advice is asked for, given, and responded to. Highlighted phrases show the target language.

📖 Situation: Youssef is stressed about his Bac results
YOUSSEF
Hey Leila, I really don't know what to do. I failed my mock exam and the Bac is in three weeks. What do you think I should do?
LEILA
I understand how you feel. First of all, don't panic. If I were you, I would start by making a study timetable. That way, you can cover every subject without feeling overwhelmed.
YOUSSEF
A timetable sounds good. But could you give me some advice on how to stay focused? I get distracted really easily.
LEILA
You should turn off your phone while you study. Also, why don't you study in short sessions of 45 minutes with 10-minute breaks? It's much more effective than studying for hours at once.
YOUSSEF
Thanks, but I've tried the Pomodoro technique before and I find it hard to stop when I'm in the zone. Is there anything else I could do?
LEILA
You might also want to join a study group. Working with others can really help. And I strongly advise you to see your teacher — I'm sure they can give you extra guidance.
YOUSSEF
That's a great idea! I appreciate your advice. I'll definitely try all of that. Thank you, Leila.
🔎 Analysis — Dialogue 1
❓
Asking for advice: "What do you think I should do?", "Could you give me some advice on…?", "Is there anything else I could do?"
💡
Giving strong advice: "You should turn off your phone", "I strongly advise you to see your teacher"
🌿
Giving soft advice: "Why don't you study in short sessions?", "You might also want to join a study group"
🚫
Qualifying advice: "Thanks, but I've tried that before…" — politely explaining it doesn't suit him
Dialogue 2

Focus: You'd better & It's high time

Read carefully and identify where these two structures appear and why they are used.

📖 Situation: Karim has been ignoring his health and his responsibilities at school
OMAR
Karim, you've been skipping PE classes and eating junk food every day. Have you got any idea how bad this is for you?
KARIM
I know, I know… but what do you think I should do? I just don't have the motivation to change.
OMAR
Listen, it's high time you took your health seriously. You've been saying "I'll start tomorrow" for months now. That needs to stop.
KARIM
You're right, but it's hard to know where to begin. Could you give me some advice on how to get started?
OMAR
First, you'd better stop eating fast food every day — your energy levels are terrible because of it. Why don't you start by cooking simple meals at home?
KARIM
That makes sense. What about exercise? Any ideas?
OMAR
You ought to go back to PE classes — the teacher has already noticed your absences. And honestly, it's high time you had a proper routine. Even a 20-minute walk every morning would help.
KARIM
I hear you. Thanks, but I'm not sure I can manage mornings. I'm really not a morning person.
OMAR
Then go in the evening. But look — you'd better not leave this any longer. The Bac is coming and you need to be in good shape mentally and physically. I strongly advise you to make one small change this week and build from there.
KARIM
You're absolutely right. I appreciate your advice. I'll start with the food this week and add exercise next week.
🔎 Analysis — Dialogue 2
⏰
It's high time you took… / It's high time you had… — past simple after "it's high time + subject" signals the action is long overdue. The speaker is mildly reproaching Karim for not having acted sooner.
⚠️
You'd better stop… / You'd better not leave this… — "had better" warns of a negative consequence if Karim doesn't act. Notice the negative form: you'd better not (no auxiliary verb added).
🌿
Soft advice: "Why don't you start by cooking simple meals?", "Even a 20-minute walk would help"
🚫
Refusing advice: "Thanks, but I'm not sure I can manage mornings" — Karim politely declines and gives a reason, which leads Omar to offer an alternative.
